The problem Core

Support towers fail in three predictable ways

Every L1/L2 tower already owns a ticket system. The model rests on one assumption — that L1 absorbs the majority of demand so L2 is spent only where expertise is genuinely required — and each failure below is a variation on that assumption quietly breaking.

Ticket ping-pong
L1 forwards without troubleshooting. L2 bounces it back as under-diagnosed. The ticket accumulates handoffs and no progress, and nobody is accountable between the bounces.
L1 cannot prove its value
Ask what share of demand L1 absorbs and nobody can answer from data. At budget time L1 reads as a switchboard with a headcount — and gets cut, which raises L2 load and worsens everything else.
SLA reports nobody trusts
Clocks that run while a ticket waits on the customer make every team look like it is failing. Leadership stops opening the report, and with it goes the only feedback loop the tower had.
Shadow support
When raising a ticket is slower than messaging an engineer directly, users message the engineer. That work is invisible — uncounted, unplanned, unprotected by any SLA. The metrics improve while the service degrades.
Knowledge never flows back
L2 solves something hard, writes a comment, closes. L1 never learns it and the same ticket escalates next month. L1's resolution ceiling stays capped at whatever its people already knew.
L1 attrition closes the loop
If every interesting ticket escalates, L1 is a dead-end job. People leave with their known-error knowledge, and first-time resolution resets with each new hire. This is what makes the other failures permanent.

Every explanation on the left is locally reasonable, which is exactly why the structural cause survives years of well-intentioned effort — training, hiring and escalation policy all get tried first, because they follow from it. The failure is not that nobody has worked at this. It is that the work has been aimed one level too high.

The design decision Core

Tier is a workflow state. Tower is a field. It is one project.

The instinct is to give L1 and L2 separate projects, or separate queues that hand off between them. That instinct is the root cause. A handoff mints a new ticket ID, restarts the SLA clock, and splits the audit trail — which is exactly why ping-pong stays invisible, why L1's contribution can't be counted, and why MTTR can't be computed end to end.

Instead, escalation is a workflow transition that flips a Support Tier field from L1 to L2 and reassigns to the tower's group. Same ticket key. Same clock, running continuously across the boundary. One history showing every hand that touched it.

Tower — Network, Database, Applications, EUC, Cloud, Security — is a single-select field, not a project. It drives queue routing and every dashboard cut. Splitting towers into their own projects is worth it only when they are separate vendors under separate contracts who must not see each other's tickets.

Those tower names are placeholders — a generic infrastructure split, invented for illustration. Replacing them with the real domains is the cheapest change available here and the largest single gain in credibility.

The mechanism Core

Enforce the discipline in configuration, not in policy

“L1 must troubleshoot before escalating” is a policy, and policies erode under queue pressure. So the rule gets moved into the workflow itself: a validator on the In Progress L1 → Escalated to L2 transition that refuses to move the ticket until the fields below are filled.

In Progress L1  →  Escalated to L2 Workflow validator
[ ] Escalation Reason — single-select, required. Becomes the category axis of the escalation report.
[ ] Troubleshooting Performed — paragraph, required, minimum length enforced. Stops one-word escalations.
[ ] KB Article Checked — required. Where the article was missing, that gap becomes the next KB backlog item.
Post-function on success: set Support Tier = L2, clear the assignee so the ticket lands in the tower queue rather than on one named person.

Do not gate a major incident

A gate that is right for a password reset is actively harmful during an outage. Forcing an analyst to compose a write-up while the business is stopped trades downtime for paperwork — so there are two escalation transitions, not one.

TransitionValidatorsWho can use it
Escalate to L2 All three fields required Any L1 agent
Escalate — major incident none — immediate Major Incident Manager role only

Role-restricted, not priority-conditional. Native Jira conditions branch on role, group and permission rather than on a field value, so gating the fast path by role works without a paid app. It is also the better design: a fast path should be a deliberate, accountable act by a named incident manager, not a free choice available to anyone feeling time pressure.

Nothing is lost, only deferred. A second validator on Resolved → Closed requires the same three fields on every ticket regardless of route. Majors skip the gate on the way in and pay it on the way out — when the incident is over and the write-up is more accurate anyway.

The gate does two jobs. It blocks the lazy escalation outright, and it manufactures the data for escalation rate per analyst — the single number that tells you whether L1 is working, and precisely who needs coaching.

Two more rules exist for the same reason. Priority is derived from an Impact × Urgency matrix rather than picked by the agent, which ends priority inflation. And SLA clocks pause on Pending Customer and Pending Vendor — the detail that makes the SLA report defensible enough that leadership keeps reading it.

Off-ramp: Cancelled Reopen within 7 days returns to Triage and flags the ticket

The reopen path matters more than it looks. Without it, a ticket closed prematurely simply reappears as a fresh ticket — which flatters first-time-resolution and hides the real failure. Routing reopens back to Triage with a flag is what makes the reopen rate trustworthy.

First-time resolution and reopen rate are deliberately paired. Closing tickets early lifts the first and wrecks the second, so neither number can be moved alone. That pairing is the whole reason the scoreboard can be trusted.

The value Core

The argument holds without a spreadsheet

Every unit of demand L1 absorbs is a unit L2 does not pay for, and L2 capacity is the scarce, expensive resource. That is the whole economic case, and it does not need a model to stand up.

There is deliberately no ROI figure here. Producing one would mean inventing a per-ticket cost, a baseline resolution rate and a monthly volume — none of which has been measured for this organisation. A fabricated model invites argument about the inputs instead of the mechanism, and it is the likeliest thing in a pack like this to fail under questioning.

The honest sequence is instrument, measure a baseline through the pilot, then quantify. The pilot produces real numbers in two weeks. Anything offered before that is decoration. The build itself is configuration against an existing Jira licence, not new software spend.

Blocker Core

Nothing below can be built until Service Management is provisioned

Checked against the live instance: only jira-software is licensed today and the /rest/servicedeskapi/ endpoints return 403. That matters, because five of the capabilities this design leans on are Service Management features and have no equivalent in Jira Software.

CapabilityService ManagementJira Software

Agent queuesnative✗ approximated with filters
Customer portal & email intakenative✗ none
Approvals for access & changenative✗ workflow hack

This is a hard prerequisite, not a caveat. Without provisioning, the SLA engine, queues and portal do not exist — which means the design cannot be built, and no part of a live walkthrough can be rehearsed. Every downstream step depends on clearing it first.

The free tier is understood to cover three agents with the complete SLA engine, which would be enough to build and prove the entire design before anyone signs a purchase order. That tiering is unconfirmed on this account and is the first thing to check — if it turns out the SLA engine sits behind a paid tier, the approach needs rethinking rather than adjusting.

Delivery Core

Eight phases, roughly eight weeks, with a working system well before the end

01Decidewk 1 Licensing call, confirmed tower list, one project or several.
02Foundationwk 1 Company-managed project, groups, roles, permission scheme.
03Schemawk 2 Custom fields, four issue types, screens, field configurations.
04Workflowwk 3 Statuses, transitions, the escalation gate validator and post-functions.
05SLA & queueswk 4 Targets with pause conditions, one queue per tower, breach views.
06Automationwk 5 Priority derivation, tower routing, P1 alerting, breach warnings, auto-close.
07Pilot — one towerwk 6–7 Where SLA targets and the escalation gate get tuned against real traffic, and the first Problem records become KB articles. Not a formality.
08Roll outwk 8+ Remaining towers, seeded with the KB the pilot produced.

Structure is scripted. Rules are exported JSON. SLA config is UI.

Fields, issue types, screens, statuses, workflows and validators are all reachable through the Jira REST API — verified against the live instance — so the structural build is scriptable, version-controlled and reproducible rather than a sequence of undocumented clicks nobody can replay in six months.

Two honest exceptions. Automation rules have no public Cloud REST API (/rest/api/3/automation/rules returns 404) — they are built in the rule builder and version-controlled through Automation's JSON export/import. SLA targets and queues are configured in the Service Management UI. That division holds for every real Jira build, and it is worth saying out loud rather than claiming full automation.

Risk Core

The gate is the thing most likely to be routed around

If the escalation gate is the mechanism the whole design rests on, it is also the thing under the most pressure. The realistic failure is not agents refusing to use it — it is agents discovering a different transition that reaches L2 without passing through it.

Mitigation: the pilot's escalation-rate data exposes the workaround within days, because one analyst's numbers will diverge sharply from the rest. Transition permissions get tightened before rollout, while the blast radius is one tower.

Open questions before build: the licensing call; the real tower list; team size and shift pattern (which decides 24×7 versus business-hours calendars); and the intake channels to wire up.
